01 — Customer types

Why six personas, and why these six?

Most CX frameworks treat all customers identically. That's the first mistake. Behaviour data from 3,000+ interactions revealed six distinct patterns — each requiring a fundamentally different response strategy, not just a different tone.

01 / 06
♥
Loyal advocate

Identified through repeat purchase rate + NPS score above 8. These customers refer others — losing them silently costs 3–5x their own LTV. Standard service isn't enough; they require relationship-mode interactions.

Key signal: High tenure, low complaint frequency, referral activity
Nurture & reward
02 / 06
⚡
Frustrated escalator

Characterised by high-emotion language, repeat contacts on the same issue, and rapidly escalating requests. Without a defined de-escalation protocol, agents default to apology loops — which makes this customer type significantly worse.

Key signal: 3+ contacts on same issue, raised tone, demand for manager
De-escalate fast
03 / 06
🏷
Deal seeker

Arrives at support often with pre-framed complaints designed to trigger compensation. Identified through discount request frequency and complaint-resolution patterns. Not malicious — but requires a firm-and-fair framework to avoid training them to complain for rewards.

Key signal: Complaint language mirrors offer terms, frequent coupon requests
Value-focused comms
04 / 06
★
First-timer

New customers contacting support within their first 30 days. Statistically, a poor first support experience has a 60% churn correlation. The methodology here is onboarding-first — resolve the issue AND reduce future friction through education.

Key signal: Account age <30 days, first or second contact ever
Onboard & educate
05 / 06
⚠
At-risk churner

Predictive signals: declining login frequency, cancelled subscription attempts, or mentions of competitor names. This persona requires proactive outreach, not reactive resolution. Waiting for them to contact you is too late.

Key signal: Usage drop >40%, subscription pause attempt, low recency
Retention priority
06 / 06
◌
Silent dissatisfied

The most dangerous type. They don't complain — they simply leave. Identified through CSAT non-response, low engagement post-issue, and zero support contact following known service failures. Proactive outreach is the only intervention that works.

Key signal: No CSAT response, no contact after known incident, low recency
Proactive outreach
02 — Escalation framework

How the four tiers were decided

The tier count itself was a design decision. Too few tiers create bottlenecks at the top; too many create confusion about where a ticket belongs. Four was the right number for a 12-person team.

The escalation architecture was built around two constraints: response time expectations and decision authority. Every tier has both — agents shouldn't be resolving issues they're not empowered to decide on, and managers shouldn't be handling issues a trained agent can close in minutes.

The SLA targets at each tier weren't guesses. They were reverse-engineered from customer expectation data: what response time, at what tier, causes a customer to escalate their complaint publicly (social media, review platforms) or abandon entirely.

Tier 3 exists specifically for policy exceptions — situations where the right answer for the customer falls outside the standard rulebook. Without a designated tier for this, agents either over-compensate (costly) or under-compensate (churn risk). Team leads at Tier 3 hold the authority to make judgment calls.

Tier 4 was scoped narrowly on purpose: legal risk, PR exposure, and executive accounts. Elevating more than 2–3% of contacts to this level signals a systemic problem upstream, so it also functions as a diagnostic metric.

1
Front-line agent
Standard queries, order status, product questions, basic account issues
SLA: 15 min Chat + Email ~78% of volume
2

Legal exposure, PR risk, executive accounts, systemic failures

03 — Compensation logic

What drove the compensation decisions

Compensation design is where most CX frameworks go wrong. They either over-compensate (training customers to complain) or under-compensate (failing to recover goodwill). The logic here was built on three principles.

Match the emotional weight

Compensation should match how wrong the customer feels — not just what went wrong. A refund for a defective product is expected. A handwritten apology and a thoughtful upgrade for a loyal customer who waited three weeks for a wrong order — that's memorable. The method changes the feeling.

Never train bad behaviour

If the same compensation (e.g. 20% discount) is offered for any complaint regardless of severity, customers learn that complaining has a fixed payout. The matrix was designed so that compensation scales with genuine harm — not just persistence or vocal volume.

Strengthen the relationship

For high-value customers, the best compensation deepens loyalty rather than just fixing the transaction. A loyalty tier upgrade costs less than a full refund — but generates more goodwill and retention impact.

Measure the outcome, not the cost

Every compensation decision is logged against the customer's subsequent retention, NPS change, and repeat purchase rate. Over time, this creates empirical data on which methods actually work — not just which feel most generous in the moment.

04 — Process

How the framework was built

The system wasn't designed top-down. It was built from observed patterns — then stress-tested against edge cases before being codified into training and tooling.

01
Contact audit

3,000 historical tickets were tagged by outcome, channel, customer segment, and agent. Patterns in resolution time, repeat contacts, and CSAT correlation were mapped.

02
Persona clustering

Behaviour clusters were identified through frequency, tone, and resolution data. Six archetypal patterns emerged consistently across ticket categories and channels.

03
Protocol design

Each persona received a handling playbook: opening approach, escalation triggers, compensation authority, and follow-up cadence. All tested in simulated role-plays.

04
Training & rollout

Agents completed 6 training modules across 4 weeks. Performance was tracked weekly. Playbooks were updated based on real-world edge cases within the first month.

"The goal was never to resolve tickets faster. It was to make customers feel like the company actually understood them — and then prove it with a response that matched that understanding."
— Design principle, CX Command Center
